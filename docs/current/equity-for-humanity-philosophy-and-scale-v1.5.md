# Equity for Humanity — Philosophy and Scale Note v1.5

> **Working draft:** This is a living philosophy and modelling note for Equity for Humanity. It is not a fundraising claim, legal opinion, securities offering, investment product, or promise of future distributions. Figures are order-of-magnitude estimates using scenario assumptions, not forecasts.

## Core thesis

Equity for Humanity is about **predistribution**: giving humanity a permanent ownership claim in the productive assets that shape its future.

As capital, AI, and automation become more central to wealth creation, every human being should have some claim on capital too — especially because AI is built from the accumulated intelligence, labour, language, creativity, culture, science, code, institutions, and knowledge of humanity.

No single company, country, investor, or generation created that inheritance alone. AI systems are trained on, shaped by, and made valuable through the totality of human contribution up to this point. Equity for Humanity expresses the idea that some share of the prosperity created from that inheritance should compound back for humanity.

This is not meant to replace work, family, creativity, public services, charity, or national policy. It is **universal supplementary ownership**: a growing, compounding support system for human security, dignity, agency, and flourishing.

> **Humanity should own part of the productive engine that shapes its future.**

## The practical goal

The long-term aspiration is universal participation: every person should eventually be able to participate and benefit if they choose. The right is always there; participation grows over time.

Equity for Humanity can grow organically across three dimensions:

1. **The fund grows** through contributions, compounding, asset donations, future AI-era value sharing, bequests, partner campaigns, and institutional support.
2. **The economy grows** especially if AI increases global output and productive-asset values.
3. **Participation grows** as people become aware of the project, trust it, verify, and choose to claim.

The model is therefore not:

> “Raise hundreds of trillions first, then begin.”

It is:

> **Start small. Prove the concept. Grow the fund. Grow the reach. Let compounding and economic growth increase the benefit over time.**

## Why claiming can be universal and naturally progressive

Equity for Humanity can remain universal without requiring everyone to claim immediately.

> **Universal by right. Opt-in by choice. Progressive by use. Self-recycling by design.**

People with greater need are more likely to claim early benefits. People who do not need the benefit may ignore it, donate it back, or allow it to recycle into the endowment.

That means E4H does not need a heavy means test to balance universality with practical need. The balance is intended to emerge organically:

- people who need the benefit are more likely to claim;
- people who do not need it are more likely to let it recycle;
- unclaimed amounts return to the endowment;
- the universal right remains intact without forcing people to prove poverty or worthiness.

## Access challenge

The people in deepest poverty may also be among the hardest to reach at the beginning. Some may lack reliable smartphones, internet access, government ID, bank/mobile-money accounts, or safe local institutions.

So the early path may not perfectly match the deepest need. Equity for Humanity may first reach people who are easier to verify and connect, then deliberately improve access for people who face the greatest barriers.

That makes the **Connect** part of the model important. Local initiatives, nonprofits, schools, faith communities, community groups, and future verification partners can help expand access over time.

> **Claiming should become easier over time, especially for people who need the benefit most.**

## Scale of the challenge

Current global order-of-magnitude figures:

- **World GDP, 2024:** about **US$111 trillion**.
- **World population, 2024:** about **8.14 billion** people.
- **Global listed-company market capitalization, 2025:** about **US$141 trillion**.
- **Listed public-equity market value per human:** about **US$17,350**.

A useful benchmark is **20% of basic poverty-level needs**. Using rough international poverty-line benchmarks:

| Benchmark | 20% support target |
|---|---:|
| 20% of $3/day extreme-poverty line | about **$219/person/year** |
| 20% of $4.20/day lower-middle poverty line | about **$307/person/year** |
| 20% of $8.30/day upper-middle poverty line | about **$606/person/year** |

If every human being on Earth claimed, the fund required would be very large:

| Universal annual target | Fund needed at 1% payout | Fund needed at 3% payout |
|---|---:|---:|
| $219/person/year | **~$178T** | **~$59T** |
| $307/person/year | **~$250T** | **~$83T** |
| $606/person/year | **~$493T** | **~$164T** |

This clarifies the ambition. E4H is not an ordinary charity. A universal ownership dividend that materially supports basic needs requires a fund measured in **tens to hundreds of trillions** if it is truly global. But it does **not** require reaching everyone in year one.

## Investment and growth assumption

The intended investment reference is not a narrow company, sector, or single ETF. It is closer to a broad global index approach, such as the **FTSE Global All Cap Index** or a similar globally diversified benchmark that owns productive companies across countries, sectors, and market sizes.

The goal is not to pick AI winners. It is to own productive global growth broadly, efficiently, and transparently as AI, automation, and capital become more central to wealth creation.

For base-case modelling, a broad global-equity assumption around **8% annual growth** is reasonable as a scenario input, with clear caveats. In an AI buildout scenario, growth may be higher and more volatile.

## Dynamic payout rule

The strongest current candidate is a **dynamic payout rule**:

> **Participant payout = 3% for every 10% of average growth, using a 5-year trailing average.**

Examples:

| 5-year average growth | Participant payout rate |
|---:|---:|
| 0% or negative | 0% payout |
| 3% growth | 0.9% payout |
| 5% growth | 1.5% payout |
| 10% growth | 3.0% payout |
| 20% growth | 6.0% payout |
| 30% growth | 9.0% payout |

For the first years, the average can be seeded with a broad global-equity benchmark assumption until the fund has five years of its own history. For example:

- year 1: four prior benchmark years plus the current year;
- year 2: three prior benchmark years plus two actual years;
- year 3: two prior benchmark years plus three actual years;
- year 4: one prior benchmark year plus four actual years;
- year 5 onward: five actual years.

This rule is attractive because it combines ambition with protection:

- when growth is strong, people share more;
- when growth is weak, the fund protects the future;
- if the average growth is zero or negative, participant payout falls to zero;
- the 5-year average smooths one unusually strong or weak year;
- it helps preserve the contribution base while still offering meaningful support.

> **When shared prosperity grows faster, people share more. When growth slows, the fund protects the future.**

## Charity-disbursement context

The legal payout rule may not be entirely optional. In Canada, registered charities are subject to a **disbursement quota** based on relevant investment/property assets. The commonly cited current rule is **3.5% on the portion up to $1 million and 5% on the portion above $1 million** of property not used directly in charitable activities or administration. Exact application depends on structure and legal advice.

That does not necessarily mean every dollar must be paid as direct participant cash benefits. Some required spending could potentially support eligible charitable activities, access, verification, administration, education, community support, or other mission-aligned charitable purposes, depending on the legal structure.

So the payout design should distinguish between:

1. **Legal charitable disbursement requirement** — what the organization must spend to comply with charity law.
2. **Participant benefit payout rule** — what share of the fund is distributed directly to participants.
3. **Recycling/unclaimed amounts** — benefits not claimed may remain in, or return to, the charitable/public-benefit structure depending on the final design.

This note is not legal advice. The final structure needs professional advice.

## Modelling approach

The model tracks:

- annual return;
- contribution share of world GDP;
- cumulative contributions;
- fund value after payouts;
- active claimants;
- payout per person;
- fund strength: **ending fund ÷ cumulative contributions**.

The key sustainability test is:

> **When does the fund rise above cumulative contributions and stay above them after payouts?**

That is the point where compounding is visibly adding value above what contributors put in.

The model uses a volatile but AI-positive return path:

> +30%, +18%, −12%, +35%, +22%, −8%, +28%, +15%, +10%, −18%, +25%, +12%, +8%, −10%, +16%, +8%, +6%, −12%, +18%, +7%, then lower but still volatile growth.

Contributions are modelled as a share of world GDP. They start around **$1.2M** in year 1, grow as the economy grows, reach about **0.5% of world GDP**, then taper toward **0.35%** by year 30.

## Four payout scenarios

### Scenario 1 — Fixed 1% payout: safest but too small

A fixed 1% payout preserves the fund, but does not provide enough support soon enough. It is too conservative for the mission.

### Scenario 2 — Fixed 3% payout: meaningful but barely sustainable

A fixed 3% payout gives people more money, but in this model it only reaches break-even around year 30. It provides support, but leaves less margin for future compounding.

### Scenario 3 — Dynamic 3× payout, 5-year average: proposed rule

The dynamic rule pays more when growth is strong and less when growth is weak. It smooths payouts using a 5-year average and preserves the fund above cumulative contributions while still producing meaningful support.

### Scenario 4 — Fixed 5% payout: warning case

A fixed 5% payout gives the highest payments in the short run, but fails the sustainability test. It pays too much from the fund and ends below cumulative contributions.

| Fixed 3% | 30 | ~$32 | ~$66 | ~$291 | ~$892 | ~$1,596 | ~$2,217 | 1.00× |
| **Dynamic 3×, 5-year average** | 24 | ~$59 | ~$35 | ~$302 | ~$499 | ~$617 | ~$808 | 1.17× |
| Fixed 5% | Not reached | ~$53 | ~$109 | ~$471 | ~$1,408 | ~$2,426 | ~$3,218 | 0.86× |

This table shows the tradeoff clearly:

- **Fixed 1%** protects the fund, but the payments are too small.
- **Fixed 3%** pays more, but only barely preserves the contribution base by year 30.
- **Dynamic 3× with a 5-year average** is the best current candidate: smoother, responsive, contribution-preserving, and meaningful.
- **Fixed 5%** pays the most, but is not sustainable because the fund ends below cumulative contributions.

The dynamic rule does not guarantee payouts rise every single year. In weak return periods, payouts may fall. That is the safety feature. But over the long term, as contributions, the economy, and the fund grow, the goal is an upward trajectory in participation and support.

## Proposed rule in plain language

> **Equity for Humanity should distribute participant benefits dynamically: 3% for every 10% of average growth, using a 5-year trailing average. If average growth is zero or negative, participant payout falls to zero.**

This means:

- people share more when shared prosperity grows faster;
- the fund protects itself when growth slows;
- one bad year does not dominate the payout;
- contributors can see their money preserved and compounded;
- the system can aim for meaningful human support without sacrificing the future.

## Philosophy in one paragraph

Equity for Humanity exists to build a permanent, globally diversified public-benefit endowment whose returns give people a growing share of global prosperity. Its purpose is not to replace human work or ordinary public policy. Its purpose is to establish universal supplementary ownership: a compounding human floor funded by shared ownership of productive assets. Because AI is built on humanity’s accumulated intelligence, culture, language, labour, science, and creativity, humanity has a moral claim on some share of the prosperity AI helps create. If AI causes the economy to grow dramatically, humanity should not merely hope for redistribution after the fact. Humanity should hold a permanent ownership claim in the productive engine shaping its future.

## Working language to preserve

- **Predistribution, not only redistribution.**
- **Humanity should own part of the productive engine that shapes its future.**

- **The first dollar proves the concept. The compounding grows the floor.**

- **Universal participation remains the long-term moral horizon.**

- **If AI creates immense wealth, ownership decides who benefits.**

## Open questions

1. Is a 5-year trailing average the best smoothing period, or should the model test 3-year, 5-year, and 7-year averages across harsher cycles?
2. How should unclaimed benefits recycle back into the fund?
3. What legal structure best supports universal aspiration while meeting charity, tax, securities, and governance requirements?
4. What share of future AI-era wealth could plausibly be contributed without being burdensome relative to global GDP?
5. How quickly can access barriers be reduced for people who are hardest to reach?
6. What governance would make ordinary people trust that the fund belongs to humanity, not founders, donors, governments, or vendors?

## Sources and modelling notes

- World Bank indicators used for order-of-magnitude grounding: world GDP, world population, world GDP per capita, and market capitalization of listed domestic companies.
- Global listed market capitalization is only a partial measure of productive wealth; it excludes private companies, land, infrastructure, human capital, public assets, and other forms of wealth.
- Poverty-line support targets are simplified annualized benchmarks: **20% × daily line × 365**.
- Fund-size estimates use: **required fund = annual benefit × claimants ÷ payout rate**.
- Scenario tables are illustrative, not forecasts. They subtract participant payouts from fund balances and are meant to show how reach, fund growth, economic growth, payout policy, cumulative contributions, volatility, contribution-ramp assumptions, and claim behaviour interact.
- All figures are approximate and should be updated as the concept, data, and legal structure mature.
