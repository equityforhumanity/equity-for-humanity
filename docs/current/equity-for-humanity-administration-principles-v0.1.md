# Equity for Humanity — Administration and Stewardship Principles v0.1

**Status:** Working draft for public review  
**Purpose:** Define how Equity for Humanity will administer resources fairly, sustainably, transparently, and with best-in-class efficiency.  
**Core idea:** Equity for Humanity will be strong, not fragile; hyper-efficient, not extractive; agentic-first, but human-accountable.

---

## 1. North star

Equity for Humanity exists to build a permanent public-benefit endowment so every human being can share in a growing portion of global prosperity.

Administration will exist only to protect and strengthen that mission.

> **The fund exists for humanity. Administration exists to steward it responsibly.**

The goal is not merely to meet ordinary charitable overhead expectations. The goal is to demonstrate a better model:

> **Best-in-class public benefit with the lowest sustainable administrative burden.**

---

## 2. Administration doctrine

Equity for Humanity will be designed around these principles:

1. **Agentic first, human accountable** — AI agents will do as much administrative, research, drafting, reporting, translation, monitoring, and support work as safely possible. Humans will remain accountable for judgment, approvals, governance, legality, and trust.
2. **Strong, not fragile** — Low overhead matters, but not at the cost of compliance, security, auditability, participant protection, governance, or long-term sustainability.
3. **Transparent by default** — Administrative costs, stewardship costs, compensation, platform costs, fundraising costs, participant distributions, reserves, and reinvestment will be categorized and reported in plain language.
4. **Budgeted and reviewed** — Administration will be budgeted, reviewed, and justified. Growth in the Humanity Fund will not automatically create uncontrolled growth in administration.
5. **Independently governed** — Founder/steward compensation and major operating policies will be approved through independent governance, with conflicts disclosed and managed.
6. **Mission capital is protected** — Resources committed to the Humanity Fund will never become private benefit for insiders or operating capital for the organization.
7. **Self-sustaining over time** — The project will aim to support necessary stewardship costs without relying on fragile personal sacrifice or constant emergency fundraising.
8. **Surplus returns to humanity** — Stewardship resources not needed for approved costs, prudent reserves, or an approved stewardship sustainability reserve will return to the Humanity Fund.

---

## 3. Early-stage reporting

In the early phase, administrative percentages may look high because there may be little or no participant payout yet. The project may have concept, legal, prototype, governance, and infrastructure costs before significant distributions are possible.

To avoid misleading comparisons, Equity for Humanity will report early-stage activity separately, including:

1. **Build and incubation costs** — concept development, legal exploration, governance design, prototype work, and public materials.
2. **Humanity Fund contributions** — resources committed to long-term compounding and participant benefit.
3. **Participant distributions** — benefits actually distributed to verified participants when distributions begin.
4. **Stewardship and operations costs** — governance, compliance, platform, reporting, verification, and support.
5. **Reserves** — resources held for stability, approved future costs, and risk management.

Early percentages will not be allowed to shame the project before it has scale. Instead, the project will clearly distinguish between temporary build costs and mature operating efficiency.

Long-term, as the Humanity Fund grows, the administrative percentage should fall.

---

## 4. The two-bucket model

For trust, Equity for Humanity will use two visible buckets.

### Bucket A — Humanity Fund

**Purpose:** The permanent public-benefit endowment for humanity.

Used for:

- long-term investment and compounding;
- participant benefits;
- reinvestment;
- recycled unclaimed benefits;
- mission-locked public-benefit capital.

Principle:

> **Money given to the Humanity Fund is for humanity: compounding, participant benefit, and permanent shared ownership.**

Bucket A will be kept pure. It will not be used for administration, operations, founder compensation, staff compensation, board costs, fundraising, platform costs, legal costs, or organizational overhead.

No founder, steward, board, donor, advisor, staff member, contractor, or future governance body will have authority to redirect Bucket A for ordinary operations. If stewardship resources are unavailable, the project will slow down, pause, simplify, or seek Bucket B support. It will not draw from Bucket A.

Plain-language rule:

> **Bucket A is for humanity, not administration. If stewardship cannot be funded through Bucket B, the work must become leaner, slower, or better supported — not funded from Bucket A.**

### Bucket B — Stewardship and Operations Reserve

**Purpose:** The necessary support system that keeps the mission lawful, trustworthy, secure, durable, and effective.

Used for:

- legal advice and regulatory compliance;
- accounting, audit, tax, and reporting;
- governance and board support;
- platform hosting, security, and technical operations;
- participant verification systems;
- privacy, safety, and fraud prevention;
- communications and transparency reporting;
- agent/tool infrastructure;
- reasonable founder, staff, or contractor compensation approved under policy;
- insurance and risk management;
- prudent operating reserves;
- an approved stewardship sustainability reserve.

Principle:

> **Stewardship funds exist to protect the mission, not to compete with it.**

Bucket B will be transparent, budgeted, reviewed, and reported. If Bucket B accumulates more than is reasonably needed for approved operating costs, prudent reserves, and an approved stewardship sustainability reserve, the surplus will be transferred to Bucket A.

---

## 5. Flow of funds

Equity for Humanity will use a clear flow-of-funds model:

1. **Humanity Fund contributions** go to Bucket A.
2. **Stewardship contributions** go to Bucket B.
3. **Unclaimed participant benefits** recycle back to Bucket A.
4. **Unused stewardship surplus** returns to Bucket A after approved costs, prudent reserves, and stewardship sustainability needs are covered.
5. **Participant distributions** are made from the Humanity Fund under the approved payout policy.
6. **Stewardship costs** are paid only from Bucket B.

This creates a clear trust promise:

> **Every dollar is labelled. Every dollar has a purpose. Bucket A stays pure. Surplus stewardship returns to humanity.**

---

## 6. Donor and operations funding strategy

Equity for Humanity will seek to make ordinary mission contributions as powerful as possible.

Where appropriate, the project will engage major donors, institutional supporters, and aligned partners to help cover stewardship and operating costs so that more public and grassroots contributions can go directly to the Humanity Fund.

This model will be pursued carefully and transparently:

- operations support will be disclosed as stewardship funding;
- stewardship funding will not buy control over the Humanity Fund;
- donor restrictions or conditions, if any, must be compatible with the mission and transparently communicated;
- donors will not receive control over participant benefits, payout rules, investment policy, governance independence, or the purity of Bucket A;
- administration will be budgeted and reviewed;
- the long-term aim is a self-sustaining model, not permanent dependence on a small group of funders.

Principle:

> **Major supporters can help carry the cost of stewardship so more contributions can compound for humanity. Over time, the system should become increasingly self-sustaining.**

---

## 7. Stewardship sustainability model

Equity for Humanity will seek to make Bucket B increasingly self-sustaining over time.

A possible mature model is a **Stewardship Sustainability Reserve** inside Bucket B. This reserve would exist only to support future stewardship needs. It would not be part of Bucket A and would not dilute the purity of the Humanity Fund.

The reserve could be managed under rules such as:

- maintain a prudent operating runway for approved stewardship costs;
- invest longer-term stewardship reserves in a simple, low-cost, globally diversified portfolio, similar in spirit to the Humanity Fund’s boring broad-market approach;
- use a conservative, smoothed spending rule so stewardship can be supported without draining the reserve too quickly;
- report the reserve separately from the Humanity Fund;
- transfer excess stewardship resources to Bucket A when they are not needed for approved costs, prudent reserves, or long-term stewardship sustainability.

This creates a durable pattern:

> **Bucket A compounds for humanity. Bucket B sustains the stewardship required to protect Bucket A. Surplus Bucket B flows back to Bucket A.**

If Bucket B cannot support a proposed activity, Equity for Humanity will not take from Bucket A. The activity must be delayed, simplified, funded by new stewardship support, or not pursued.

---

## 8. Sustainability and compensation safeguards

Equity for Humanity should not depend on any founder, steward, staff member, or volunteer burning out, working indefinitely for free, or personally subsidizing the mission forever.

Fair compensation for real work can protect continuity, accountability, and quality. The project, however, will use strong safeguards to prevent private benefit, conflicts of interest, or extraction from the Humanity Fund.

Founder, steward, staff, or contractor compensation will be:

- for actual work performed;
- reasonable and modest in early stages;
- capped where practical at the compensation-policy level;
- approved through independent governance;
- benchmarked when possible;
- publicly disclosed in plain language where legally and practically appropriate;
- reviewed annually;
- subordinate to the mission.

The founder will recuse himself from any decision on his own compensation.

Equity for Humanity will not use compensation structures that create a private claim on the Humanity Fund. The founder will not take:

- a percentage of Humanity Fund donations;
- a percentage of participant distributions;
- an automatic percentage of assets under management;
- uncapped or undisclosed related-party payments;
- compensation approved only by himself.

Plain-language principle:

> **Stewards may be compensated for real stewardship work, but never in a way that gives them a private claim on humanity’s fund.**

---

## 9. Best-in-class efficiency targets

These are working targets, not legal promises.

Early-stage ratios can be misleading because build, legal, governance, and prototype costs may occur before meaningful participant distributions. Early reporting will therefore separate build/incubation costs from mature operating ratios.

| Stage | Administration / stewardship target |
|---|---:|
| Concept and prototype | Report separately; no meaningful mature ratio yet |
| Pilot stage | Aim for under 15–20% where possible |
| Early operating institution | Aim for 10–15% |
| Mature endowment | Aim for under 5% |
| Large-scale aspiration | Stretch goal of 1–3% |

Long-term principle:

> **As the Humanity Fund grows, the administrative percentage should fall.**

The goal is to become a proof of concept for ultra-efficient public benefit: a model where almost all economic value either compounds for humanity or reaches participants.

## 10. Agentic administration model

Equity for Humanity will use agents to reduce cost and increase quality, while preserving human accountability.

Agents can help with:

- research and policy comparison;
- drafting and editing public documents;
- translation and localization;
- donor and participant FAQs;
- dashboard summaries;
- financial modelling;
- routine reporting drafts;
- software prototyping;
- accessibility review;
- meeting preparation;
- monitoring public feedback;
- preparing governance materials.

Humans will retain authority over:

- legal structure;
- custody and investment decisions;
- public fundraising claims;
- participant eligibility and verification rules;
- privacy and child/youth protections;
- compensation decisions;
- risk acceptance;
- public commitments;
- final approval of reports and disclosures.

Principle:

> **Agents reduce the burden. Humans carry the responsibility.**

---

## 11. Public reporting categories

To avoid confusion and build trust, Equity for Humanity will report spending in clear categories:

1. **Humanity Fund capital** — resources preserved and invested for humanity.
2. **Participant benefits** — distributions claimed by verified participants.
3. **Recycled benefits** — unclaimed or voluntarily returned benefits reinvested.
4. **Stewardship and operations** — necessary administration, compliance, platform, reporting, and support.
5. **Stewardship sustainability reserve** — Bucket B resources held or invested to support future mission protection and operations.
6. **Fundraising and outreach** — costs of raising resources and expanding participation.
7. **Founder/staff/contractor compensation** — disclosed separately where legally and practically appropriate.
8. **Reserves** — stewardship funds retained for approved future costs, stability, or risk management.

Simple dashboard language:

> **Compounded for humanity. Distributed to participants. Recycled for the future. Used for stewardship. Reserved for resilience. Returned to humanity when surplus.**

---

## 12. Governance safeguards

Before accepting significant public funds, Equity for Humanity will develop governance safeguards such as:

- independent board or advisory oversight;
- conflict-of-interest policy;
- compensation policy;
- expense reimbursement policy;
- investment/custody policy;
- reserve policy;
- public reporting policy;
- privacy and participant-protection policy;
- annual review of administrative burden;
- plain-language explanation of how Bucket A and Bucket B work;
- an explicit rule that Bucket A cannot be used for administration or private benefit.

Minimum governance test:

> **Could an ordinary donor, participant, regulator, or journalist understand where the money goes and why?**

---

## 13. Public promise draft

> Equity for Humanity will be agentic-first, human-accountable, and best-in-class in administrative efficiency. Resources for the Humanity Fund will be mission-locked for long-term compounding and participant benefit. Bucket A will not fund administration, operations, compensation, fundraising, or organizational overhead. Resources for stewardship will be held separately in Bucket B and used only to protect, operate, govern, and report on the mission. Stewardship costs will be transparent, independently overseen, and designed to fall as a percentage over time. Surplus stewardship resources will return to the Humanity Fund so more value compounds for humanity.

Shorter version:

> **The Humanity Fund is for humanity. Stewardship exists to protect it. Bucket A stays pure. Bucket B sustains the work. Surplus stewardship returns to humanity.**

---

## 14. The trust test

An administration model is aligned with Equity for Humanity if the project can honestly say:

1. Bucket A is used only for humanity: compounding, reinvestment, participant benefit, and recycled benefits.
2. Administration protects the mission rather than feeding itself.
3. Stewardship costs are paid only from Bucket B.
4. Founder/steward compensation is reasonable, disclosed, independently approved, and conflict-managed.
5. No insider has a private ownership claim on the Humanity Fund.
6. Costs are categorized in plain language.
7. Agents are used to reduce overhead while preserving accountability.
8. Surplus stewardship returns to the Humanity Fund when not needed for approved costs, prudent reserves, or stewardship sustainability.
9. As scale grows, the administrative percentage falls.

---

## 15. Working conclusion

Equity for Humanity will aim to be:

> **Best-in-class, not self-sacrificial. Hyper-efficient, not fragile. Founder-stewarded, not founder-owned. Agentic-first, human-accountable. Strong enough to last, lean enough to trust.**

This is part of the proof of concept: showing that modern tools, transparent governance, and mission-locked design can benefit humanity without a huge administrative burden.
